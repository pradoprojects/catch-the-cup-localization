## 📌 Overview

**Catch the Cup** started as a simple question:

> What happens when thousands of people share the same emotional moment without necessarily sharing the same language?

Inspired by the 2026 FIFA World Cup — hosted across the USA, Canada, and Mexico — this project explores how music, subtitles, language, and timing can create a shared experience across multilingual audiences.

Instead of treating localization as a final production step, I approached language as part of the experience itself.

The project combines:
- multilingual songwriting
- subtitle-driven UX thinking
- AI-assisted creative iteration
- transcreation
- emotional pacing
- audiovisual synchronization

What started as a localization experiment slowly became something closer to experience choreography.

---

## 🌍 Three Countries. One Crowd.

The 2026 World Cup creates an interesting design constraint:

- 🇺🇸 USA  
- 🇨🇦 Canada  
- 🇲🇽 Mexico  

Three host countries.  
Three dominant language contexts.  
One shared event.

Instead of separating languages into isolated versions, I explored how English, French, and Spanish could coexist inside the same experience while still feeling cohesive, rhythmic, and emotionally readable.

The Brazilian Portuguese layer appears through subtitles as a transcreation rather than a direct translation.

## 🎬 Subtitle-Driven UX Thinking

I wanted the subtitles to feel less like captions and more like part of the performance.

The PT-BR layer was written specifically for real-time audiovisual consumption, with attention to:
- reading rhythm
- line segmentation
- pacing
- repetition
- synchronization
- cognitive load during faster sections

Some lines were rewritten entirely just to breathe better on screen.

In several moments, rhythm and emotional clarity mattered more than literal equivalence.

## ⚡ Working with AI

This project became one of my most iterative experiments with AI-assisted workflows.

AI made it possible to quickly test:
- different lyrical structures
- pacing variations
- subtitle timing
- emotional tone shifts
- multilingual combinations
- alternative musical directions

Some ideas worked immediately.  
Others completely fell apart.

The interesting part was realizing that AI helped me move faster — but not decide faster.

Human judgment still mattered constantly:
- Which lines felt emotionally clear?
- Which subtitles were readable under timing pressure?
- Which language combinations felt natural instead of chaotic?
- Which moments actually felt memorable?

At some point, I became oddly obsessed with subtitle rhythm.

The hardest part of this project was not translation.

That’s the part I’m still thinking about.
